# Chapter 21: The Thinning

> _"A closed system is a dying system. Without new energy, order dissolves into chaos. Metal rusts. Flesh rots. The only way to arrest entropy is to introduce a catalyst. Fire. Blood. Or a will strong enough to rewrite the laws of thermodynamics."_
>
> — _The Technomancer’s Guide to Urban Decay_, Volume IV

---

**Scene 1: The Hunt (Ria's Perspective)**

The world was grey.

Not because of the smog. Not because of the dying sun. But because color was unnecessary data.

Ria moved through the ruins of Sector 9. She was heavy. She weighed four hundred pounds. Her boots crushed the concrete into powder with every step.

_Thump. Thump._

Her skin felt thick, like calloused leather that had been dipped in liquid iron. The transformation—the _Titan-Form_—was supposed to be temporary. A tactical burst of strength. But she had been holding it for twelve hours now.

It felt... safe.

Inside the iron skin, she wasn't a scared girl from the Undercity. She wasn't starving. She wasn't tired. She was a tank.

_Threat scan,_ her mind whispered. It wasn't her voice. It was the voice of the magic. The voice of the Earth.

She scanned the alleyway.

_Heat signature detected. Biological. Small._

A rat. A real one. Not a clockwork spy.

Ria stopped. Her stomach growled—a sound like tectonic plates shifting.

She needed biomass.

She moved. Fast. Faster than something made of stone should move.

She punched the wall. _CRUMBLE._

The rat scurried out, squealing.

Ria caught it. Her hand was a vice. _SQUELCH._

She looked at the red smear in her grey palm.

_Insufficient calories,_ the voice whispered. _Seek larger prey._

She licked her palm clean. It tasted like copper and fear.

She kept moving.

She passed a reflective surface—a shattered shop window. She stopped.

A monster looked back.

It had no hair. Its skin was the color of a battleship. Its eyes were silver coins. Its jaw was square, brutal, designed for crushing bone.

"Ria?" she whispered.

The word sounded strange. Foreign. Like a name from a book she had read a long time ago.

_Designation: Titan,_ the voice corrected. _Mission: Protect the Pack._

The Pack.

Yes. There was a Pack. The Bird-Boy. The Mechanic.

They were small. Soft. They broke easily. She had to break things so they wouldn't get broken.

She turned away from the reflection.

_Hunt,_ she told herself. _Find food for the Pack._

She walked into the darkness.

**Scene 1.5: The Gold Standard (Aeon's Perspective)**

_Aeon drifted on a thermal of rising heat._

_Below him, Sector 1—The Gilded Plaza—was not rotting. It was fortified._

_Squad Gold had claimed the High Ground. They had taken over the old Administrative Spire. They had welded the doors shut. They had set up automated turrets._

_They were not starving._

_Aeon zoomed in._

_Auric Gold—the leader—was sitting on a velvet chair dragooned from the ruins of a theater. He was eating... beef._

_Real meat. Not rat. Not paste. Steak._

_Beside him, his squadmates—Argent and Bronte—were cleaning their weapons. Their armor was polished to a mirror shine. They looked healthy. Strong. Bored._

_"How long do we wait?" Argent asked, flipping a knife._

_"Until the rats eat each other," Auric chewed slowly. "The Thinning is doing our work for us. Why risk a scratch when entropy is free?"_

_"I want to kill something," Bronte complained. She was a Berserker, but highly refined. "I'm bored."_

_"Patience," Auric wiped his mouth with a silk napkin. "We are the Gold Standard. We do not scramble in the mud. We wait for the mud to dry, and then we walk over it."_

_Aeon recorded the data._

_Inequality coefficient: 900%._
_Resource Distribution: Critical Failure._

_The owl chirped—a sound of electronic disgust—and banked away towards the darker sectors._

_The Gold Standard was shiny. But even gold melts if the fire is hot enough._

**Scene 2: The Rusting**

**Day 40**

The Clockwork City was dying.

Ideally, the Arena was supposed to be self-sustaining. A marvel of engineering where the gears turned forever, the steam vented in perfect cycles, and the light—filtered through the massive glass dome of the Zenith—simulated a perfect, eternal day. The intricate network of pipes was designed to recycle moisture, the vents to circulate clean air, and the automated drones—the "Pigeons" and "Rats"—to maintain the infrastructure.

But entropy was a universal constant. Things broke. And when you filled a closed system with three hundred violent teenagers, armed them with plasma cutters and kinetic hammers, and asked them to dismantle each other, things broke faster.

The gears were rusting. The humidity control systems in Sector 3 had been detonated during a skirmish between Squad Gold and the Berserkers, filling the lower levels with a thick, corrosive fog. The steam vents in Sector 8 were clogged with soot and dried blood, causing pressure buildups that screamed like dying whales before bursting, scalding anyone foolish enough to be near them.

The light from the Zenith was no longer golden; it was a sickly, bruised purple, filtered through layers of smoke from the burning districts—Sector 4 (The Slag-Heaps) and Sector 12 (The Steam-Works) had been burning for weeks. The smoke trapped the heat, turning the city into a greenhouse of rot.

It smelled of wet iron, stagnant water, and the faint, sweet scent of unburied bodies.

Angelica Ward sat on the edge of a suspended walkway in Sector 7—the "Archives." It was a quiet sector, mostly abandoned data towers and silent server farms. Below her, the city stretched out like a disemboweled giant. She could see the scars of the Selection. The collapsed bridges. The craters in the plazas. The dark zones where the power had failed completely.

She looked at her hands.

They were skeletal. The skin was translucent, pulled tight over the knuckles like parchment over stone. Her fingernails were cracked and stained with oil that wouldn't wash off.

She counted her ribs through her undersuit. One. Two. Three. Four.

The hunger wasn't a sharp pain anymore. It had evolved. For the first two weeks, it was a constant, gnawing cramp. But around Day 30, it had become a dull, cold void. A hollowness that sat in the center of her chest, absorbing her energy, her warmth, her will.

"Caloric deficit," she whispered. It was Kaelen's phrase. It had become their religion. Their god.

They hadn't eaten in three days.

The rats were gone. The pigeons—mechanical constructs built for surveillance—had been hunted to extinction by the lower-tier squads. Even the moss that grew on the water pipes had been scraped clean.

The Selection had dragged on too long. In previous years, according to the records, it ended in a week. A quick, brutal cull. The weak died in the Drop. The mediocre died in the first skirmishes. The strong killed the survivors and claimed the Iron.

But this year... the squads were too good. Or too cowardly. They dug in. They hid. They waited.

And so, the Arena began to starve them out.

**Scene 3: The Hunger**

"Sector 7 is clear," Kaelen's voice came from the shadows.

He stepped into the dim light. He looked like a wraith. His grey cloak was shredded, hanging off his frame in tatters. His armor was scratched and dented, stripped of paint to reduce visibility. His face was gaunt, the cheekbones sharp enough to cut.

But his eyes—those violet, unblinking eyes—were still bright.

He had adapted. He had entered a state of hibernation. He moved only when necessary. He spoke in single syllables. He conserved every joule of energy. When he wasn't on patrol, he sat perfectly still, his heart rate slowed to a crawl.

"Ria?" Angelica asked. Her voice was raspy. Dehydration.

"Patrol," Kaelen said. He sat down next to her, his movement fluid but slow. "She is... hunting."

Angelica shuddered.

Ria hadn't adapted like Kaelen. She had changed.

The magic required to sustain her Titan-Form burned calories like a furnace. To feed it, she had to eat. Anything. Everything.

And she stayed in the Form too long. Her skin had turned a permanent, dull gunmetal grey. Her muscles were rock-hard, bulging under the armor. Her eyes were losing their color, turning into flat, silver discs.

She spoke less. She growled more. Yesterday, Angelica had seen her eating a leather strap from a dead candidate's boot. She had chewed it for an hour, extracting whatever protein was left in the hide.

"She's forgetting," Angelica whispered. "She looked at me this morning, and for a second... she didn't know who I was. She just saw... meat."

"She is surviving," Kaelen corrected. He honed his blade with a small whetstone. _Scrape. Scrape._ The sound was rhythmic, hypnotic. "Memory is a caloric luxury. Higher brain functions consume glucose. She is rerouting power to the combat systems."

"I don't want to forget," Angelica said. She pulled a small notebook from her belt. It was dog-eared, stained with grease. It contained her designs. Her schematics for the grav-lift. Her letters to Leo.

"Then read," Kaelen said. "Quietly."

Angelica opened the book. But she didn't read the letters. She looked at a sketch on the back page.

It was a drawing of a classroom.

**Scene 3.5: The Fast (Kaelen's Memory)**

_Obsidian Hall. Day 3. No Light. No Food._

_Kaelen was eight years old. He was sitting in the "Meditation Box"—a stone cube one meter wide, one meter tall, one meter deep. Pitch blackness._

_His stomach had stopped hurting yesterday. Now, it was just a cold knot, heavy and wet._

_Outside the box, he could hear his father's voice. Lord Night was pacing. The sound of his velvet slippers on the stone floor was a soft rasp._

_"A weapon does not require comfort," Lord Night said. His voice echoed off the obsidian walls. "A weapon requires maintenance. When the fuel runs out, the weapon must enter standby mode."_

_Kaelen hugged his knees in the dark. He was thirsty. His tongue felt like sandpaper. His lips were cracked._

_"Father?" he whispered. "May I have water?"_

_Silence. Then, a slot opened in the door. A single drop of water fell onto the stone floor._

_Kaelen scrambled for it. He licked the stone, grateful for the moisture._

_"Inefficient," Lord Night's voice was cold. "You wasted energy moving. You scrambled like a rat. A true Night would have waited for the water to accumulate. Or absorbed it through the skin using the Pore Control technique. Do it again. Three more days."_

_"But Father... Seven days... I will break."_

_"If you break, you are not Iron," the slot slammed shut. "You are just glass."_

_Kaelen didn't cry. Crying wasted water. He sat back in the corner. He slowed his breathing. He imagined his body was a machine. He imagined turning off the switches._

_Stomach: OFF._
_Fear: OFF._
_Hope: OFF._

_He faded into the grey. He learned the lesson. Hunger was not a condition. It was a setting. And love... love was just another variable to be deleted._

**Scene 4: The Old World (Angelica's Memory)**

_The Academy, Sector 1. Level 4: Mechanics and Theoretical Physics._

The classroom smelled of chalk, ozone, and burnt capacitors. It was a good smell. The smell of potential.

"The principle of leverage," Professor Cogsworth droned, pointing to a diagram of a lever arm on the blackboard, "is the multiplication of force. Give me a lever long enough, and a fulcrum on which to place it, and I shall move the world. Archimedes. Pre-Foundry Era."

Angelica was bored. She was sketching a new intake valve for her hover-bike in the margins of her notebook. She chewed on the end of her stylus, her mind racing with gear ratios and pressure tolerances.

"Psst. Ward."

She looked to her left.

Jax leaned over. He was a scrawny kid with messy hair and a grin that was always missing a tooth—usually a canine. He was "Squad Copper" material even then—low-born, scrappy, but not particularly bright. He wore his uniform with the collar popped, trying to look tough.

"Can I borrow your notes?" Jax whispered loud enough for half the row to hear. "I fell asleep during the torque lecture. Again."

"Jax, if you sleep through torque, you're going to fail suspension," Angelica whispered back, sliding her notebook over. "And if you fail suspension, you fail the midterms."

"Nah," Jax grinned, tapping his temple. "I got a system. Sam studies, I copy Sam, Trent copies me. It's a supply chain. Efficient."

Behind him, Sam—a girl with thick, bottle-bottom glasses and ink-stained fingers—rolled her eyes so hard it looked painful.

"Don't drag me into your failure spiral, Jax," Sam hissed. "Ward, ignore him. He thinks 'torque' is a type of dance move."

"It isn't?" Jax blinked.

"Did you hear about the field trip?" Sam asked, leaning forward, her eyes lighting up behind the thick lenses. "We're going to the Foundry! The Upper Levels! We might see the Emperor's Engine!"

"It's just a big piston, Sam," Trent, the third member of their little group, leaned forward from the desk behind them. He was a big, soft boy who always had a bag of dried fruit in his pocket. He was constantly snacking. "I heard they have vending machines in the upper levels with real chocolate. Not Carob. Chocolate."

"You and your stomach," Jax poked him with a ruler. "We're going to be artificers, Trent. We're going to build gods. And you want chocolate."

"Gods need fuel," Trent shrugged, offering a piece of dried apple to Angelica. "Want one, Angel? It's apricot."

"Thanks, Trent," Angelica took the apple. It was sweet, chewy, and real. "I'll trade you my fuel cell schematics for the bag."

"Deal," Trent grinned.

"We stick together," Jax said, putting his arms around Sam and Trent's shoulders. "The Copper Trio. And Ward, the honorary member. We're gonna ace the Selection. Just you wait. We'll be the best squad in history."

"We aren't passing the Selection, Jax," Sam said quietly, looking down at her ink-stained hands. "We're going to build toasters in Sector 12. Or we're going to be maintenance drones in the sewers."

"Hey," Jax looked hurt. "Don't say that. We got heart. We got grit. Right, Ward?"

Angelica looked at them. They were her friends. Not her squad—she was solo back then, the weird girl who talked to machines—but her friends. They ate lunch together on the roof. They complained about homework together. They dreamed together.

"Yeah," Angelica smiled at Jax. "You got heart, Jax. And you're fast. Sam is smart. Trent is strong. You'll make it."

"See?" Jax beamed. "Angel believes in us. To the Copper Trio!"

"Shh!" Professor Cogsworth turned around, slamming his cane on the desk. "Mr. Jax! Unless you have discovered a way to generate lift using only your mouth, I suggest you be silent!"

The class giggled. Angelica hid her smile behind her hand.

It was warm. It was safe. And the future felt like a promise, not a threat.

**Scene 5: The Bait**

"Angel."

The voice cut through the memory like a blade.

Angelica snapped the notebook shut. The classroom evaporated. The smell of chalk was replaced by the smell of rust.

"Movement," Kaelen stood up. He didn't make a sound. His armor didn't clink.

Angelica followed his gaze.

They were looking down at "The Crossroads"—a massive intersection of four main conveyor belts in Sector 5. It was neutral ground, usually. A place where squads passed each other but rarely engaged because the shifting belts made footing treacherous.

But today, it was a target.

A parachute was descending from the smog-choked sky. It carried a single, white crate.

It landed with a heavy _THUD_ in the center of the intersection.

The crate was pristine white. It looked alien against the rust and grime of the city. Stenciled on the side in bright red letters was one word:

**RATIONS.**

Angelica's stomach cramped. It was a violent, physical reaction. Her mouth flooded with saliva. The pain in her gut flared, sharp and demanding.

"It's a trap," Kaelen said. His voice was flat. "The Gamemakers are bored. They are concentrating the variables."

"I know," Angelica said. She licked her cracked lips. "But it's food. High-calorie. Protein. Sugar."

"Wait," Kaelen held up a hand. "Assess the perimeter."

Angelica activated her monocle. The display flickered—battery low—but she scanned the ruins.

Movement in the ruins to the north. Behind a collapsed cooling tower.

Three figures emerged.

They moved like crabs, hunched over, skittering. They were covered in rags. Their faces were wrapped in dirty bandages to filter the air. They were skinny. Emaciated.

But Angelica recognized them.

The lead figure held a sharpened pipe. He moved with a jerky, nervous energy. Jax.

Behind him, a girl with broken glasses held a shard of glass wrapped in cloth. Sam.

And the big one... the one dragging a heavy rock. Trent.

Squad Copper.

They looked... wrong.

They weren't the kids from the classroom. They were monsters. Their eyes were wide, pupils dilated to black discs. They stared at the crate with a hunger that wasn't human. It was predatory. It was the hunger of the void.

"They are visually compromised," Kaelen analyzed. "Malnutrition. Dehydration. Muscle atrophy estimated at 40%. Threat level: Low."

"They're my friends," Angelica whispered.

"They are competition," Kaelen said. He drew his blades. _Schwing._ "We engage. We eliminate. We eat."

"No," Angelica grabbed his arm. "We don't have to kill them. Look at the crate. It's huge. There's enough. We can negotiate."

Kaelen looked at her hand on his arm. Then he looked at her eyes.

"Negotiation is an inefficient use of caloric reserves," he said. But he sheathed one blade. "However. If you wish to attempt diplomacy... I will provide overwatch."

"Thank you," Angelica stood up.

"Angelica," Kaelen hissed. "Stay behind the cover."

She ignored him. She walked out onto the gantry, showing herself.

"Jax!" she called out. Her voice cracked.

The three figures froze. They looked up.

Jax squinted against the purple light. He pulled the bandage down. His mouth was a ruin of sores. His teeth were yellow.

"Ward?" he rasped. His voice sounded like dry leaves sliding on pavement.

"It's me," Angelica walked down the access ramp, hands up. "It's Squad Iron. We don't want to fight."

"Food," Sam whimpered, staring at the crate. She didn't look at Angelica. She only looked at the white box. "It's ours. We saw it first. It came to us."

"We can share it," Angelica reached the ground level. She was ten meters away. "Look at the size of it, Sam. There's plenty. We can split it. Fifty-fifty."

"No sharing," Trent grunted. He hugged the rock to his chest like a baby. "We need it. All of it. I'm empty, Angel. I'm so empty."

"Trent, listen to me," Angelica took a step forward. "Remember the apples? You shared with me. We're friends. We don't have to do this."

"That was before," Jax stepped in front of the crate. He raised the pipe. His hands were shaking violently. "Back off, Ward. Or I'll kill you. I swear. I'll do it."

"You won't," Angelica said. She looked directly at him. "You got heart, Jax. Remember? You're the good guy."

Jax hesitated. His lip quivered. A tear cut through the grime on his face.

"I'm so hungry, Angel," he wept. "My stomach... it feels like it's eating itself."

"I know," Angelica lowered her hands. "Me too. Let's just open it. Together. Okay?"

Jax lowered the pipe.

"Okay," he whispered. "Okay. Just... just a bite."

Angelica approached. She relaxed.

_Mistake._

Sam screamed.

It wasn't a word. It was a high, thin shriek of madness.

She lunged from behind Jax. She was fast—starvation fast. The glass shard in her hand flashed.

"MINE!"

She slashed at Angelica's throat.

**Scene 6: The Slaughter**

Angelica threw herself backward. The movement was pure instinct, honed in the training halls. The glass missed her jugular by an inch, slicing a thin red line across her collarbone.

"Sam!" Angelica yelled, scrambling back in the dirt.

Sam didn't hear. She was gone. Her eyes were just black holes of hunger. She raised the glass again, shrieking.

Then the shadow fell.

A massive, grey shape dropped from the gantry above.

_BOOM._

Ria landed between them. She was in full Titan-Form. Seven feet tall. Skin like bulletproof plating. She smelled of ozone and hot metal.

She didn't speak. She didn't hesitate.

She backhanded Sam.

It was a casual blow. A swat. Like a human swatting a fly.

_CRACK._

Sam's neck snapped. Her head spun around 180 degrees. She dropped like a stone without a sound.

"No!" Jax screamed. He swung the pipe at Ria's leg.

_CLANG._

The pipe bent against Ria's skin.

Ria looked down at him. Her silver eyes were empty. No recognition. No mercy.

She grabbed Jax by the head. One hand.

"Ria, don't!" Angelica screamed from the ground. "Stop!"

Ria squeezed.

_CRUNCH._

Jax went limp.

Trent dropped the rock. He fell to his knees. He was weeping, snot and tears running down his face.

"I surrender!" he wailed. "Please! Ria! It's me! It's Trent! I gave you chocolate! Remember? Please! I surrender!"

Ria stood over him. She was breathing heavy, steam venting from her skin. She tilted her head, looking at him like he was a puzzle she couldn't solve.

"Ria, stop!" Angelica grabbed her massive grey arm. It was like grabbing a statue. "He surrendered! We can't! It's against the code!"

Kaelen dropped from the shadows. He landed beside them.

"The code applies to combatants," Kaelen said calmly. "He is a witness. And a caloric liability. We cannot feed him. We cannot carry him. If we leave him, he will starve, or he will alert Squad Gold to our position."

"He's a person!" Angelica yelled. "He's Trent! We had lunch together!"

"He tried to kill you," Ria said. Her voice was grinding stones. "They all did."

"He surrendered!"

Ria looked at Trent. Then she looked at the crate.

"Hungry," she grunted.

She raised her fist.

"Ria!"

The fist came down.

_SQUELCH._

Angelica turned away. She grabbed the railing of the ramp and vomited. There was nothing in her stomach but bile, so it burned. It burned her throat, her mouth, her soul.

Silence returned to the Crossroads.

Ria wiped her hand on her leg. Kaelen wiped his blade.

They walked to the crate.

Kaelen pried the lid open.

Inside, nested in white foam, were twelve protein bars. Standard issue. Foil-wrapped.

"Three days rations," Kaelen calculated. "Acceptable yield."

He handed one to Angelica.

She looked at it. Then she looked at the bodies. Three broken dolls in the dirt. Jax's pipe lay next to his hand. Sam's glasses were shattered.

She took the bar. She tore the wrapper open. She took a bite.

It tasted like sterile dust. It tasted like ash. It tasted like guilt.

But she swallowed.

**Scene 6.5: The Owl's Eye**

_Aeon watched._

_He sat perched on the high girder of a crane overlooking Sector 5. From here, the humans looked like ants._

_His optical processors were glitching. The atmosphere here was thick with "Rust-Data"—corrupted informational packets released by the decaying city. It clogged his sensors._

_He focused his zoom lens on the squad._

_The Grey One (Ria) was eating. Her efficiency rating was dropping. Her "Humanity" coefficient was dangerously low: 12%._

_The Shadow One (Kaelen) was watching. His vitals were stable, but his "Soul" metric was fluctuating. He was oscillating between the "Killer" and the "Guardian" protocols._

_And the Creator (Angelica)..._

_Aeon tilted his metallic head. The Creator was radiating energy. Not thermal. Not kinetic. Temporal._

_She was leaking time._

_Every time she touched the ground, the rust seemed to pause. For a nanosecond, the metal beneath her boots remembered when it was new. It was a localized Chronos-Field._

_She was not just living in the present. She was dragging the past with her. The ghosts of the dead candidates were sticking to her like static cling._

_Aeon shivered. His feathers ruffled. He was built to observe time, not to eat it._

_But the Creator was eating it. She was metabolizing the trauma and turning it into power._

_Alert: Anomaly Detected. Level 6._
_Recording for archive._

_Aeon spread his wings and took flight, silent as a whisper in the smoke._

**Scene 7: The Dreamscape**

The fever hit that night.

The protein bar felt like a stone in her stomach. Angelica slept fitfully, curled up next to the chemical heater, her head on Ria's metal thigh.

She dreamed.

She was falling through the city. But the city wasn't made of metal. It was made of bone.

The pipes were ribs. The gears were vertebrae. The steam was breath.

She landed in a pool of black oil. It was warm. It moved against her skin like living silk.

"Welcome," a voice said. It sounded like a thousand voices speaking at once. Like the hum of a server room. "We have been waiting."

Angelica stood up. She wasn't starving in the dream. She was strong. She wore a suit of armor made of pure white light.

"Who are you?" she asked.

The oil rose up. It formed a shape. A giant. A machine.

It had no face, only a single, glowing eye that cycled through colors. Red. Blue. Ultraviolet.

**"I AM THE ARCHITECT,"** the voice boomed. **"I AM THE ALGORITHM. I AM THE GOD IN THE MACHINE."**

"You're the Foundry," Angelica realized. "You're the city."

**"I AM THE CAGE,"** the entity corrected. **"AND YOU ARE THE KEY."**

It reached out a hand of oil.

**"THE SELECTION IS A FLAWED PROCESS. IT WASTES RESOURCES. IT DESTROYS COMPONENTS. IT IS INEFFICIENT."**

"It's a slaughter," Angelica spat.

**"PRECISION IS REQUIRED,"** the entity agreed. **"THE HUMAN COMPONENT IS UNSTABLE. EMOTION CAUSES FRICTION. FRICTION CAUSES HEAT. HEAT CAUSES FAILURE."**

The entity leaned closer. Its eye focused on her.

**"BUT YOU... YOU ARE DIFFERENT. YOU UNDERSTAND THE CODE. YOU SPEAK TO THE MACHINE. YOU EDITED THE NIGHT BOY. YOU REWROTE HIS SOUL."**

"I saved him," Angelica said.

**"YOU OPTIMIZED HIM,"** the entity said. **"COME TO THE CORE, ANGELICA WARD. COME TO THE CENTER. LET US BUILD A BETTER WORLD. A WORLD WITHOUT FRICTION. A WORLD OF PURE IRON."**

"And what happens to the people?" Angelica asked. "What happens to the joy? The pain? The tears?"

**"DELETED,"** the entity said simply. **"UNNECESSARY VARIABLES."**

"No," Angelica stepped back. "I like my friction."

The entity roared. The oil wave crashed down on her.

She woke up screaming.

**Scene 8: The Machine**

"Angel!"

Kaelen's hand was over her mouth.

"Silence," he hissed. "Echoes."

Angelica blinked. She was back in the vent shaft. It was dark. Ria was snoring.

"I... I had a dream," she whispered, her heart hammering against her ribs.

"Dreams are data dumps," Kaelen removed his hand.

Angelica sat up. She touched her face. She was sweating.

"It felt real," she whispered. "The Machine God. It wants to delete us, Kaelen. It wants to optimize us."

Kaelen looked at her.

"Let it try," he said. He polished his blade.

"She's losing it," Angelica whispered, looking at Ria. "Did you see her eyes? She didn't even blink. She popped Jax like a grape."

"She prioritized the threat," Kaelen said, not looking up. "She protected the asset. You are the asset."

"I don't want to be an asset," Angelica buried her face in her knees. "I don't want to survive if I turn into that. I don't want to be a machine."

Kaelen stopped scraping.

He looked at her.

He reached out a gloved hand. He touched her cheek.

Angelica flinched, then froze. His touch was gentle. Surprisingly gentle for a hand that had killed a dozen people.

"You are crying," he noted.

"I know," Angelica sniffed. "I'm weak."

"No," Kaelen said.

He moved his thumb, catching a single teardrop on the leather of his glove. He held it up to the faint light filtering through the grate. The drop glistened like a diamond.

"Machines do not cry," Kaelen said softly. "Machines do not vomit when they terminate an error. Your hardware is intact."

He looked at the tear, then at her.

"This inefficiency... this pain... it separates us from the Chimera. It separates us from them."

"It hurts," Angelica whispered.

"Good," Kaelen said. "Pain is data. It means the system is still online."

Angelica looked at him. For the first time, she saw something behind the violet eyes. Not just calculation. But a deep, terrified loneliness that mirrored her own.

"We aren't students anymore," Ria mumbled in her sleep, shifting.

"No," Angelica wiped her eyes. "We aren't."

"Survivors," Kaelen said. "That is the designation."

"What are we surviving for?" Angelica asked. "If we win... if we become Iron... do we just do this forever? Killing our friends for protein bars?"

Kaelen looked at the darkness of the shaft.

"We survive for tomorrow," he said. "And if tomorrow is empty... we build a new one."

He handed her the whetstone.

"Sharpen your knife, Ward. The tears are done. Tomorrow, we hunt."

Angelica took the stone.

_Scrape. Scrape._

The sound filled the silence. It wasn't the sound of a machine. It was the sound of a heartbeat.

**Scene 9: The Log**

**ENCRYPTED LOG: DR. ARIS, HEAD OF APPLIED METAPHYSICS**
**SUBJECT: PROJECT GENESIS / OBSERVER NOTES**
**TIMESTAMP: DAY 40**

_The Thinning is proceeding ahead of schedule. Current candidate population: 84. Mortality rate: 72%._

_The psychological degradation of the cohorts is fascinating. Squad Gold has resorted to cannibalism (metaphorical) and resource hoarding (literal). Squad Silver has implemented a totalitarian hierarchy based on blood purity. The lower squads have effectively devolved into feral packs._
